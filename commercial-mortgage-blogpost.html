Commercial real estate (CRE) financing differs significantly from residential mortgages. Whether you're buying office space for your business, purchasing an investment property, or acquiring a warehouse for operations, understanding CRE financing is essential for making informed decisions and securing favorable terms.

This comprehensive guide covers everything from property types and loan structures to qualification requirements and strategies for first-time CRE buyers.

What is Commercial Real Estate Financing?

CRE financing refers to loans secured by commercial property‚Äîbuildings used for business purposes rather than residential living. These loans are used to purchase, refinance, or develop commercial properties.

Key Differences from Residential Mortgages:

  • Higher Down Payments: 20-35% vs. 3-20% residential
  • Shorter Terms: 5-25 years vs. 30-year residential standard
  • Higher Rates: Typically 1-2% above residential rates
  • Focus on Property Income: Property's ability to generate revenue matters more than personal income
  • More Complex Underwriting: Property evaluation, tenant leases, market analysis

Types of Commercial Real Estate

CRE is categorized into several property types, each with different financing characteristics:

Office Buildings

  • Class A: Premium buildings, best locations, newest
  • Class B: Older but well-maintained, good locations
  • Class C: Older, requiring renovation, less desirable areas
  • Typical LTV: 70-80%

Retail Properties

  • Strip malls, shopping centers, standalone retail
  • Tenant quality heavily influences terms
  • Typical LTV: 65-75%

Industrial & Warehouse

  • Manufacturing facilities, distribution centers, flex space
  • Often easier to finance due to multiple-use potential
  • Typical LTV: 75-80%

Multi-Family (5+ units)

  • Apartment buildings, student housing
  • Most reliable cash flow, easiest CRE to finance
  • Typical LTV: 75-80%

Special Purpose

  • Hotels, gas stations, car washes, churches
  • More difficult to finance due to specialized use
  • Typical LTV: 60-70%

CRE Loan Structures

Traditional Commercial Mortgage

Structure: Fixed or variable rate, amortized over 20-30 years, balloon payment at 5-10 years

Example: $1M loan, 6.5% rate, 25-year amortization, 10-year term = $6,752 monthly payment, $735,000 balloon at year 10

Best For: Stable properties with long-term ownership plans

SBA 504 Loan

Structure: 50% bank loan, 40% SBA loan (CDC), 10% down payment

Terms: 10-25 years, below-market rates, owner-occupied only

Best For: Owner-occupied properties, first-time CRE buyers

Bridge Loan

Structure: Short-term (6-36 months), higher rates (8-15%), interest-only payments

Best For: Renovation projects, quick acquisitions, transitional properties

Construction Loan

Structure: Draw-based funding, interest-only during construction, converts to permanent loan

Best For: Ground-up construction, major renovations

Qualification Requirements

Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)

The most critical metric in CRE lending:

DSCR = Net Operating Income √∑ Annual Debt Service

Minimum Required: 1.20-1.35x (property income is 120-135% of debt payments)

Loan: $1M at 6.5%, 25-year amortization = $81,024 annual payment

This exceeds lender minimums and demonstrates strong coverage.

Loan-to-Value (LTV) Ratio

  • Owner-Occupied: Up to 85% LTV (SBA 504)
  • Investment Property: 65-80% LTV typical
  • Special Purpose: 60-70% LTV

Down Payment Requirements

  • SBA 504: 10% owner-occupied
  • Conventional: 20-35% depending on property type
  • Special Purpose: 30-40%

Personal Financial Strength

  • Credit Score: 680+ preferred, 650 minimum for most lenders
  • Net Worth: Many lenders require net worth equal to loan amount
  • Liquidity: 6-12 months reserves after closing
  • Experience: Previous CRE ownership or industry expertise valued

The CRE Loan Process

Phase 1: Pre-Qualification (1-2 weeks)

  • Submit basic financials and property information
  • Lender provides preliminary approval and terms
  • Obtain pre-approval letter for making offers

Phase 2: Property Under Contract (2-4 weeks)

  • Execute purchase agreement with financing contingency
  • Order property appraisal ($2,000-$10,000)
  • Conduct environmental assessment Phase I ($1,500-$3,000)
  • Building inspection and due diligence

Phase 3: Full Underwriting (3-6 weeks)

  • Submit complete loan application and documentation
  • Lender reviews property financials, leases, and condition
  • Appraisal and environmental reports completed
  • Loan committee approval

Phase 4: Closing (1-2 weeks)

  • Title work and insurance
  • Attorney review of documents
  • Final walkthrough
  • Closing and funding

Total Timeline: 60-90 days from offer to closing (experienced buyers with prepared documentation can close faster)

Documentation Checklist

Property Documents:

  • Purchase agreement
  • Rent roll (existing tenants and lease terms)
  • Operating statements (3 years)
  • Current leases for review
  • Property tax bills
  • Insurance declarations
  • Utility bills
  • Maintenance records

Business/Personal Documents:

  • Business tax returns (3 years)
  • Business financial statements
  • Personal tax returns (3 years)
  • Personal financial statement
  • Business plan (if new venture)
  • Resume showing CRE experience

Tips for First-Time CRE Buyers

  1. Start with Owner-Occupied: Better terms available, qualify for SBA 504 with just 10% down
  2. Build Your Team Early: CRE broker, commercial lender, attorney, accountant, inspector
  3. Understand True Operating Costs: Account for maintenance, vacancy, management, capital improvements
  4. Focus on Location: Class B property in A location often better than Class A in C location
  5. Review Leases Carefully: Triple-net vs. gross, tenant creditworthiness, lease expiration dates
  6. Plan for Balloon Payment: Have refinance or sale strategy before balloon comes due
  7. Consider SBA 504: Lower down payment and better rates make first deal possible

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Underestimating Expenses: Properties always cost more than expected‚Äîpad your estimates
  • Ignoring Vacancy Rates: 100% occupied today doesn't mean 100% next year
  • Skipping Environmental: Environmental issues can kill deals and create liability
  • Overleveraging: High LTV leaves no cushion when issues arise
  • Poor Tenant Diversification: Single tenant properties are riskier (tenant leaves = 100% vacancy)
  • Neglecting Professional Inspection: $5,000 inspection can save you $500,000 in repairs
